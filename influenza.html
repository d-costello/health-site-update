Influenza

Influenza, or the flu, is a contagious illness caused by flu viruses

Last updated 3/30/2026

What is Influenza?

Influenza, commonly called "the flu," is a contgaious disease caused by influenza viruses. Typical influenza viruses affect the nose, throat, and lungs

Symptoms

  • Fever with or without chills
  • Dry cough
  • Sore throat
  • Runny or stuffed nose
  • Headache
  • Muscle aches
  • Extreme fatigue or tiredness
  • Sometimes vomiting and/or diarrhea (more common in children)

Compared to the common cold, influenza infection is often more severe and can cause more serious complications

Transmission

You can catch the flu any time of the year, but most cases of flu tend to occur between October and May of the following year

The flu spreads through tiny droplets sometimes before you even know you’re sick. Some people may be infected with influenza viruses but never show symptoms. Infected people spread these droplets by coughing, sneezing, or talking closely to others. Although less common, a person may contract flu by touching a surface or object that has influenza virus on it and then touching their own mouth, nose, or eyes.

People with the flu can transmit the virus between one day prior to symptoms and up to five to seven days after symptoms first arise. Some people, including children and people with weakened immune symptoms may be contagious for long periods of time.

Prevention

Getting your regular flu shot is the best way to prevent the flu

The different type of influenza viruses that can be protected against with the vaccine change every year. For more information about the current influenza season in the District, please visit the following page

Limit exposure and further transmission

  • Avoid close contact with sick individuals
  • If you are feeling sick, limit contact with others
  • If you have flu-like symptoms, limit contact and stay home for at least 24 hours after your fever has resolved
  • Cover your mouth with your elbow or a tissue when you cough or sneeze, then throw the tissue in the trash
  • Frequently wash your hands with soap and warm water
  • Clean and disinfect frequently touched objects and surfaces
  • Avoid touching your eyes, nose, and mouth
