COVID-19

COVID-19 is an infectious disease caused by the SARS-CoV-2 virus.

Last updated 3/30/2026

Most people with COVID-19 have mild symptoms, but symptoms may be serious or life-threatening, especially among older adults, people with weakened immune systems, and those with certain underlying medical conditions. Some people also develop Long COVID, where effects continue for months after infection has resolved.

COVID-19 spreads through small droplets released when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or talks. Other people can breathe in these droplets and become infected.

Symptoms

  • Fever or chills
  • Cough
  • Fatigue (tiredness)
  • Muscle or body aches
  • Recent loss of taste or smell, or altered taste or smell
  • Sore throat
  • Diarrhea
  • Headache
  • Congestion or runny nose
  • Shortness of breath (difficulty breathing)

Symptoms may appear 2–14 days after exposure. Contact a healthcare provider if symptoms do not improve or worsen.

Call a doctor or 911 right away if you or someone you know has

  • Trouble breathing
  • Pain or pressure in the chest
  • New mental confusion
  • Inability to wake or stay awake
  • Lips, nails, or skin appearing pale, gray, or blue

Get vaccinated

The best way to protect yourself from contracting COVID-19 is by getting vaccinated. Staying up to date with COVID-19 vaccinations helps prevent severe illness.

Reduces severe illness

Vaccination significantly decreases the risk of severe illness if you contract COVID-19.

Lowers hospitalization and death risk

Staying up to date helps reduce the risk of severe outcomes.

Decreases risk of Long COVID

Regular vaccination may reduce the likelihood of long-term symptoms after infection.

Supports community protection

High vaccination rates make it harder to spread the virus throughout the community.

How to get vaccinated

  • COVID-19 vaccines are available through healthcare providers and local pharmacies.
  • Call ahead to check availability, insurance coverage, and appointment requirements.
  • Residents who are homebound or need language/access support can request at-home vaccination by calling 1-855-363-0333.
  • Additional information is available through community vaccine connections and provider programs.

Test and treat early

Aside from staying up to date on vaccines, testing and taking proper precautions are important ways to help stop the spread.

  • Stay home when you are sick.
  • Avoid close contact with people who are sick.
  • Cover your mouth with your elbow or a tissue when you cough or sneeze.
  • Wash your hands frequently.
  • Clean and disinfect frequently touched objects and surfaces.
  • Improve ventilation.
  • Seek treatment if you have COVID-19 and are at high risk of getting very sick.

Frequently Asked Questions

Last updated: 8/11/2025

Are the vaccinations still effective for new patients?

Yes. The updated vaccine formula can help prevent COVID-19 disease and reduce severity if you get sick. Protection decreases over time, so staying up to date matters, especially for adults aged 65 and older, people at high risk of severe illness, and people who have never been vaccinated.

What is the existing data on COVID-19 in the District?

Current COVID-19 levels in the District are gradually increasing but remain below levels seen the prior summer. Wastewater surveillance remains an important tool for monitoring community spread, and hospitalizations remain low.

Why does COVID-19 increase in the summer?

Increased summer travel and waning immunity from fall/winter vaccination or illness have historically created increases of COVID-19 in the summer.

How do these variants compare to previous ones?

Current dominant variants (LP.8.1 and NB.1.8.1 (Nimbus)) are part of the Omicron lineage and evolved from a variant known as JN.1, which circulated widely in December 2023.

There is limited evidence that suggests the current variants are more transmissible and there is no evidence to suggest they cause more severe disease. Anecdotally, some individuals who have become ill with the NB.1.8.1 (Nimbus) variant report a severe sore throat. However, it's important to note that a sore throat is a common symptom across various COVID-19 variants, and whether Nimbus causes a more severe sore throat hasn't been definitively proven by scientific studies.

Overall, symptoms remain similar to those of earlier Omicron variants: typically mild, with onset around 2-14 days from exposure. Milder symptoms mean that most people will not experience more severe outcomes, like hospitalization, from these variants.

Are the current self-tests still accurate for the new variants?

Yes. Current self-tests are described as still detecting infection with the newer variants when used according to FDA and manufacturer directions.

Where can I find a self-test?

Self-testing kits are available for purchase at drug stores, grocery stores, and other retail locations throughout the city.

Is there a treatment?

Yes. Several medications have been approved for people at higher risk of severe illness, and treatment should begin within 5–7 days of symptom onset after prescription by a healthcare provider.

Is the health department doing anything to respond to the surge?

Public health staff continue to monitor lab-confirmed cases, hospitalizations, and wastewater surveillance in the District.
