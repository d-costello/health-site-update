Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV)

RSV is a common respiratory virus that makes people sick with cold-like symptoms

Last updated 3/30/2026

Respiratory syncytial virus, or RSV, is a common virus that affects the lungs and breathing. In most parts of the United States, RSV season happens every year during fall, winter, and spring. Most healthy people get better in one to two weeks. However, RSV can be serious, especially for infants and older adults.

In the U.S., RSV is the most common cause of bronchiolitis (inflammation of the small airways in the lung) and pneumonia (infection of the lungs) in children younger than 1 year old

Symptoms

People who get RSV usually start to show symptoms 4 to 6 days after getting infected. Common symptoms include:

  • Runny nose
  • Coughing
  • Fever
  • Decrease in appetite
  • Sneezing
  • Wheezing

In most children, RSV can also cause ear infections and symptoms of pneumonia, such as trouble breathing.

Infants

In infants, RSV may seem mild at first but can become more serious after a few days. Early symptoms include:

  • Runny nose
  • Decrease in appetite
  • Cough, which may progress to wheezing

In very young infants, RSV symptoms can look different. Some babies may only show:

  • Irritability
  • Decrease activity
  • Trouble breathing

Some infants with RSV do not have a fever

Get vaccinated

New RSV vaccines are now available but only for people at higher risk including:

  • Adults over 60
  • Pregnant women during weeks 32 through 36 of their pregnancy
  • Certain higher-risk children between 8 and 19 months
    • A new vaccine is available to prevent RSV in infants less than 6 months old

These vaccines are safe and effective. Talk with your medical provider to see which option is best for you

There may be a cost for the RSV vaccine. Contact your insurance provider to learn what is covered.

Most children contract RSV by the time they are 2 years old.

For most babies and young children, RSV feels like a simple cold. However, it can sometimes cause more serious illnesses. About 1-2% of babies are hospitalized with RSV in their first 6 months, but only a very small number of those develop life-threatening problems.

Older adults over 60 are also at higher risk from RSV

Seniors are more likely to be hospitalized or die from RSV because they often have other health problems and weaker immune systems.

RSV spreads more easily than the flu

RSV can be spread through droplets in the air when someone coughs or sneezes. The virus can also live for many hours on surfaces like doorknobs, countertops, and toys, as well as on hands. People can easily catch RSV by touching their face after touching contaminated surfaces.

Anyone can get RSV, and people usually get it several times during their life. Most adults and older children only have mild symptoms. Each year approximately 58,000 young children are hospitalized for RSV in the United States. Adults over 60 are the next most at-risk group, and RSV causes about 14,000 deaths a year.

Prevention

You can lower your chances of catching RSV by practicing good health habits. The most effective way to prevent RSV and other respiratory infections is:

Wash your hands with soap and water for 20 seconds

If soap and water are unavailable, use an alcohol-based sanitizer

Keep your hands off your face

Avoid touching your eyes, nose, and mouth with unwashed hands

Avoid close contact

Avoid contact such as kissing, sharing cups, or eating utensils with people who are ill

Cover your mouth

Cover your mouth and nose with a tissue when coughing or sneezing. Throw the tissue in the trash afterward

Clean and disinfect regularly

Sanitize frequently touched surfaces, like doorknobs and toys.

RSV can live on surfaces for hours.

Stay home

Avoid contact with others when you are sick to prevent spreading the virus to others

In child care settings,the following can help prevent RSV from spreading

  • Frequent hand-washing
  • Throwing away tissues used to wipe noses right after use
  • Cleaning and disinfecting toys between each child’s use
  • Keeping sick children together in one area and separate from children who are well

Frequently Asked Questions

Last updated: 3/30/2026

Who is most at risk for RSV?
  • Adults over 60
  • Infants
  • People who have chronic health conditions like heart or lung issues

However, RSV can cause illness in anyone. People usually catch RSV multiple times during their lifetime. Most adults and older children infected with RSV have mild symptoms.

How is RSV spread?

RSV spreads from person to person through the droplets of someone who is infected. This often happens when you touch a surface or object that has the virus on it and then touch your mouth, nose, or eyes before washing your hands. It can also be spread through close contact, like being near someone who is coughing or sneezing.

Should a person with RSV be excluded from work or school?

No. People with RSV do not need to stay home from work or school as long as they feel well enough to participate in their usual activities.

Can RSV be cured?

While there is no “cure” for RSV, vaccines and proper hygiene, such as handwashing and covering coughs, can help prevent infection and protect those at higher-risk.
